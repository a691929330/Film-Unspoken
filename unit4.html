PROGRAM UNIT

City · Capital · Discipline

A city's first impression on a newcomer is usually density and opportunity. But cities do not grow from nothing, and they have never been equally open to everyone. Like nations, they are built: by the bodies of laborers, by the logic of capital, by policy decisions, by particular visions of what a good life looks like. And those visions have not always included everyone.The three films in this unit approach that reality from three different angles. If you live in one of these cities, you are observing it, but it is also shaping you: your sense of belonging, your sense of how much space you are allowed to take up here. This unit wants to offer a chance to examine that process before it has finished with you.

Unarmed Verses

SYNOPSIS

This documentary portrays a community facing forced relocation, seen through the eyes of a perceptive twelve-year-old Black girl whose reflections on life, the soul, and art give voice to those rarely heard. Unarmed Verses is a film about the universal need for self-expression and belonging.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film follows a young girl living through the redevelopment of her Toronto neighborhood. Friends, family, poetry, empty lots, buildings not yet torn down. Director Charles Officer does not rush to explain policy or interview city planners. He keeps the camera on the small details of daily life, letting you feel a loss that moves so slowly it is almost impossible to name while it is happening.It is not the kind of leaving that happens overnight. It is the kind where things become unfamiliar one by one. You are still living there, but the place has begun to exist inside someone else's design. You still know the street, but the street no longer knows you.

Rotinonhsión:ni Ironworkers | The Indigenous Ironworkers that Built New York City

SYNOPSIS

For over 140 years, Rotinonhsión:ni (Haudenosaunee) ironworkers have helped build some of North America's most iconic structures, including One World Trade Centre. From the 1880s onward, ironworking became a principal source of employment for Rotinonhsión:ni men, and by the 1920s Kanien'keha:ka families from Ahkwesáhsne and Kahnawà:ke had established a community in Brooklyn known as "Little Caughnawaga," where women played a central role as operators of boarding houses and intermediaries for transient Indigenous workers. This animated short draws on Indigenous Studies methodologies and artistic talent to tell one chapter of that history.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film's greatest strength is that it redirects the gaze. Instead of looking up at the skyline, you are brought back to the people who built it. The film is about the Mohawk ironworkers who constructed New York's towers and bridges, but what it really does is change how you see a city. Those steel structures, so often treated as symbols of modern achievement, become something else here: evidence of Indigenous labor, migration, skill, and continuity.The film matters because it reminds us that colonial history did not stay out on remote land or in the past tense. It is built into the most modern and global urban landscapes. After watching, the lens through which you look at a city will not be quite the same.

City of Gold

SYNOPSIS

This short film by Pierre Berton revisits the Klondike gold rush, when thousands of prospectors pushed through brutal conditions to reach the gold fields more than 3000 km from the nearest city. Drawing on archival photographs, it sets the Dawson City of that era, alive with taverns and dance halls, against the quieter town that exists there today.

CURATOR'S NOTE

Colin Low and Wolf Koenig reconstructed that moment using historical photographs, developing a technique for animating still images by moving the camera across them, letting people long gone or long dead speak again. This film belongs in this unit because of the structure it reveals. A city can be rapidly produced through the logic of resource extraction and colonial expansion, built from nothing in a few years, then slowly emptied when the fever breaks, leaving behind foundations, ruins, and redrawn land. The gold rush ended. But the traces of that space remained, and so did the shape of its memory.For newcomers, the film opens up a reminder that rarely gets said directly: the city where you live now also has a history of being made. It looks solid and settled. But it too was once the product of a particular kind of hunger.
