PROGRAM UNIT

Nation · Machine · Noise

Have you ever stopped to notice the moment when you first started to feel this country? Not necessarily when you said its name or stepped off a plane, but something quieter and more ordinary. A song you found yourself humming. A gesture that started to feel automatic. A sense of how things should be, absorbed without knowing quite where it came from.When we talk about a nation, we are not only talking about laws and institutions. We are talking about a system of feeling, one that has to be constantly replayed, repeated, and performed in order to seem natural, stable, and self-evident. This unit wants to lift the lid on that machinery and let you see what is running inside. Placing these three films together is a way of saying something that academic research has long argued but that newcomers rarely have space to sit with: national identity is not something you find. It is something you are trained into. What we call "fitting in" is never just entering a country's institutional life. It is entering a system of sounds, symbols, and stories that has been running for a long time, a complete apparatus for determining what counts as "us," what counts as normal, and what is worth remembering.

Shameless Propaganda

SYNOPSIS

This documentary turns the lens on the NFB itself, examining the 500 films it produced between 1939 and 1945 as a coordinated propaganda effort. Founding Commissioner John Grierson saw documentary as a "hammer to shape society," and filmmaker Robert Lower takes him at his word. Using only those films and period photographs, Lower reconstructs the image of Canada they were designed to project, and asks how much of it we still recognize today. What he finds is alternately illuminating and unsettling.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film shows you how a state uses the camera to train emotion, build consensus, and tell people what a good citizen looks like, what to feel proud of, and who to imagine as a threat. The hardest thing about propaganda is that it makes you feel like you are feeling freely.John Grierson once said that documentary is a hammer to shape society. After watching this film, you will understand the full weight of that claim. There is also something in this film that gets more familiar the longer you watch. The wartime language of loyalty, sacrifice, and collective identity did not retire when the war ended. It changed its clothes and kept going, in other eras, through other media. You may have seen it somewhere before.

Universal Language

SYNOPSIS

Somewhere between Tehran and Winnipeg, multiple characters drift through a surreal interzone where lives intersect in unexpected ways. Two girls discover money frozen in the ice. A tour guide leads bewildered visitors through Winnipeg's landmarks. A man quits his government job and sets out to visit his mother. Space, time, and identity blur and fold into one another in a comedy of misdirection and displacement.

CURATOR'S NOTE

Matthew Rankin turns Winnipeg into a space that does not quite resemble anywhere, yet feels strangely familiar. Prairie winters, the sensibility of Iranian cinema, absurd and tender fragments of daily life: several loosely connected storylines drift through this world at an unhurried pace. The humor is real, the images are beautiful, but these are only the entry points. What sits deeper is this: language, space, and belonging have never had a fixed original version.The film shows what many theoretical texts struggle to say. "Canada" has never been a fixed answer waiting for you to step into. It is a process of translation, misrecognition, and reassembly, reimagined by different people in different languages. As a newcomer, you may recognize something in this. Living here likely means living inside a constant act of translation, between languages, between your original way of seeing and the texture of daily life in this place. The film suggests that the slippage, the disorientation, the feeling of not quite being able to pin things down, is not a problem. It is a real way of being here.

The National Scream

SYNOPSIS

Through animation and mock-documentary form, this sharp satire takes aim at Canada's long-running search for a national identity, tracing along the way how the beaver came to stand in for an entire country.

CURATOR'S NOTE

Before you watch, take a moment to think: what is Canadian national identity? The beaver? The maple leaf? Winter? The idea that Canadians are America's neighbors but not Americans? This 1980 NFB short takes that question seriously, then renders it through mock-documentary and animation as something like a gentle, bewildered breakdown. One by one, the solemn symbols of nationhood are held up to the light, turned over, and reintroduced to the audience with a mildly puzzled expression. Is the beaver as a national symbol meant sincerely? Is the anthem? What about those moments when you rise, hand on chest, and feel something collective stir inside you? Are those sincere?

For newcomers, this film has an unexpected resonance. You arrived carrying a different system. You know that a national anthem could be a different song, that a national animal could be a different creature, that the boundary of "us" could be drawn somewhere else entirely. What this film tries to make strange for local audiences, you may have felt from your very first day here. No one told you that this strangeness might be a form of insight. But it is.
