UNIT 1
PROGRAM UNIT

Land · Law · Resistance

This unit opens the festival because any honest understanding of Canada cannot begin with the institutions, laws, and identities that came after the country was established, it must begin with the land itself, and with the conflicts, governance, and resistance that have always surrounded it. For many newcomers, Canada first appears as a place of stability, legality, and order. These three films remind us that this national image was not naturally given. It was built upon centuries of land dispossession, legal discipline, and the ongoing resistance of Indigenous peoples. The unit was programmed with a particular hope that audiences, especially those of us who came to Canada as adults might recognize that coming to know this country means more than learning its present-day institutions and values. It also means understanding the earlier histories of this land, the conflicts that remain unresolved, and the Indigenous sovereignty claims that continue to this day.

Kanehsatake: 270 Years of Resistance

SYNOPSIS

In July 1990, a dispute over a golf course proposed on Kanien'kéhaka (Mohawk) lands in Oka, Quebec, escalated into a historic confrontation. Director Alanis Obomsawin spent 78 days behind Kanien'kéhaka lines filming the standoff between protestors, the Quebec police, and the Canadian army. The film won over a dozen international awards and became the first documentary to win Best Canadian Feature at TIFF. Jesse Wente has called it a "watershed film in the history of First Peoples cinema."

CURATOR'S NOTE

Many first encounter this history through the name "Oka Crisis," but what makes this film so remarkable is that Obomsawin didn't see the confrontation as breaking news. She spent 78 days filming from within the Kanien'kéhaka barricades, capturing the tension, exhaustion, and anger, but also the community's endurance and dignity. The "270 years" of the title is itself an argument: this was not an eruption but a continuation. The film changed who gets to tell history. Kanehsatake is a film recommended to every newcomer to Canada. It is also one of the ten works named to UNESCO's Canadian Cinematic Heritage list.

You Are on Indian Land

SYNOPSIS

Released in 1969, this short documentary was made by the Indian Film Crew, the first all-Indigenous unit at the NFB. It captures a protest by the Kanien'kéhaka (Mohawk) of Akwesasne, whose territory straddles the Canada–U.S. border. When Canadian authorities restricted their duty-free cross-border passage — a right established by the Jay Treaty of 1794 — protesters blocked the international bridge between Ontario and New York State. Director Michael Kanentakeron Mitchell later became Grand Chief of Akwesasne. The film screened widely across the continent, helping to mobilize a new wave of Indigenous activism, and was shown at the 1970 occupation of Alcatraz.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film drops you directly into the 1969 Akwesasne Mohawk protest as it unfolds: the bridge, the border, the police, the confrontation, all of it captured in real time. It makes "land" and "law" visible rather than abstract, and gives us the rare thing of Indigenous people speaking their own position on camera, claiming the right to be heard in their own words. For newcomers today, the film's power may lie in a feeling of recognition. For those who come from places marked by border disputes, colonial wounds, or unresolved conflicts over land, what unfolds on screen is not simply "Canadian history." It is something that resonates against lived experience. The film reminds us that the borders and sovereignties that appear settled on a map of Canada also have contested, unfinished origins.

Trick or Treaty?

SYNOPSIS

Treaty No. 9 covers a vast stretch of northern Ontario, but what it actually means remains deeply contested. To the Canadian government, it represents a surrender of Indigenous sovereignty; to the Cree descendants of its signatories, its original purpose was to share the land, not cede it. Trick or Treaty? portrays one community's efforts to enforce their treaty rights and protect their lands, while revealing the broader complexities of treaty agreements. The film made history in 2014 as the first work by an Indigenous filmmaker to screen in the Masters section at TIFF.

CURATOR'S NOTE

The film is built around Treaty No. 9, but it never feels like a history lesson, because the question at its center is one that many of us already know in some form: how does a promise come to be reinterpreted, hollowed out, or turned against its original meaning? What makes the film compelling is that it refuses to leave the treaty in the past. It shows how a document signed over a century ago continues to shape land, resources, and daily life today.

For audiences from many different backgrounds, this will not feel distant. Most of us have seen how legal language can appear neutral while quietly serving power. Watching this film is an invitation to reconsider a first impression many newcomers share: that Canada is simply a place where the rules are fair and the system works.
