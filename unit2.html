PROGRAM UNIT

Border · Body · Belonging

You may have just arrived, or you may have been here for a few years. You may already have status, or you may still be waiting. But wherever you find yourself right now, there is probably a feeling you recognize: that uncertainty about whether you truly belong here.This unit wants to sit with that feeling and ask where it comes from. It is not your fault. It is not a sign that you have not tried hard enough to fit in. It has a longer history than that.Canada presents itself as multicultural, open, and inclusive. That image is not entirely false, but it covers over something harder to look at. These three films try to bring what has been covered back into view. Belonging is not only a matter of personal adjustment or individual feeling. It is also a historical question and a political one. If you feel a certain distance today, that distance did not come from nowhere. It has a name, a genealogy, and people who carried it long before you arrived.Learning this history is not meant to leave you disillusioned. It is meant to tell you that what you feel is real, that you are not the first, and that you are not alone.

Minoru: Memory of Exile

SYNOPSIS

After the bombing of Pearl Harbor, nine-year-old Minoru Fukushima was branded an enemy of Canada. Like thousands of other Japanese Canadians, he and his family were sent to internment camps in British Columbia and eventually deported to Japan. Directed by Minoru's son Michael, the film weaves classical animation with archival material, interweaving the father's memories with the son's voice to tell a story of a birthright lost and recovered.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film holds a history of displacement inside a child's memory: the smells he recalls, the sounds, the details of a life he could never return to. Through his eyes, we do not see the grand narrative of World War II. We see a Japanese Canadian family, uprooted from their own lives by the state in a single day. Words like "expulsion," "internment," and "loss of home" read as distant concepts in a history textbook. This film makes them concrete: a child's expression, a family that does not know what they are allowed to take or where they are going.It also reminds us of something unsettling. Belonging is not secure. It can be revoked overnight, by war, by panic, by a racialized gaze. Not because you did anything wrong, but because you looked like a certain kind of person. If this film makes you uneasy, I think that unease is real and worth sitting with. It is telling you that the state has never treated everyone who lives here the same way. Understanding that history is part of living here too.

Speak It! From the Heart of Black Nova Scotia

SYNOPSIS

In a predominantly white Halifax high school, a group of Black students navigate daily racism, from slurs on bathroom walls to the absence of Black history in their curriculum. Together they form a Cultural Awareness Youth Group, working with mentors to build pride through education and cultural programs, and to find ways to create change.

CURATOR'S NOTE

Has anyone ever spoken up for you, but said something that was not quite what you meant? This film refuses that kind of mediation from the start. It does not let others explain these young people. It hands the camera to them directly, and lets them talk about anger, about accent, about their bodies, about the moments of being misread, categorized, and seen as something other than what they are. About growing up in Canada and carrying a feeling of not quite fitting, a feeling without a clear name but always present. The film was made with Black youth in Hamilton in the 1990s. But watching it, you may find that what they are saying sounds like you. It matters not only because it preserved voices from that era, but because those voices have not aged out. Belonging has never been as simple as getting status, learning the language, and finding work. It also involves a harder question: who gets to define who you are? Who is doing the naming? And do you ever get the chance to speak yourself into being, on your own terms?

In the Shadow of Gold Mountain

SYNOPSIS

Filmmaker Karen Cho travels from Montreal to Vancouver to gather testimonies from the last surviving witnesses of the Chinese Head Tax and Exclusion Act, the laws that singled out Chinese immigrants as unwanted in Canada from 1885 to 1947. Drawing on history, poetry, and personal testimony, the film illuminates an era whose consequences have shaped the identity of generations.

CURATOR'S NOTE

You may have come to Canada because you were needed. Your skills, your labor, your particular usefulness. More than a century ago, another group of people came the same way. They carved through the Rocky Mountains with their bodies and built the railway that connected this country into a single nation. Without them, Canada would look different. But when the tracks were finished, the country turned around and charged them a tax. Simply for existing. Simply because their presence was considered a problem.

This film tells that history: Chinese railway workers, the Chinese Immigration Act, the head tax. But what stays with you is not the historical facts themselves. It is the structure they reveal. The country needed their labor but would not give them a full place within it. It welcomed their work but rejected them as people. The film seems to be asking a question that still has no clean answer: why are some people always recognized as workers first, and only reluctantly, if ever, recognized as people?

Watching this as an Asian newcomer, something strange happens. The faces in those black and white photographs do not feel far away. Canada's story of multiculturalism is real, but behind it sits another story, one made of exclusion, silence, and selective forgetting.
