PROGRAM UNIT

Border · Body · Belonging

You may have just arrived, or you may have been here for a few years. You may already have status, or you may still be waiting. But wherever you find yourself right now, there is probably a feeling you recognize: that uncertainty about whether you truly belong here.This unit wants to sit with that feeling and ask where it comes from. It is not your fault. It is not a sign that you have not tried hard enough to fit in. It has a longer history than that.Canada presents itself as multicultural, open, and inclusive. That image is not entirely false, but it covers over something harder to look at. These three films try to bring what has been covered back into view. Belonging is not only a matter of personal adjustment or individual feeling. It is also a historical question and a political one. If you feel a certain distance today, that distance did not come from nowhere. It has a name, a genealogy, and people who carried it long before you arrived.Learning this history is not meant to leave you disillusioned. It is meant to tell you that what you feel is real, that you are not the first, and that you are not alone.

Minoru: Memory of Exile

SYNOPSIS

After the bombing of Pearl Harbor, nine-year-old Minoru Fukushima was branded an enemy of Canada. Like thousands of other Japanese Canadians, he and his family were sent to internment camps in British Columbia and eventually deported to Japan. Directed by Minoru's son Michael, the film weaves classical animation with archival material, interweaving the father's memories with the son's voice to tell a story of a birthright lost and recovered.

CURATOR'S NOTE

This film holds a history of displacement inside a child's memory: the smells he recalls, the sounds, the details of a life he could never return to. Through his eyes, we do not see the grand narrative of World War II. We see a Japanese Canadian family, uprooted from their own lives by the state in a single day. Words like "expulsion," "internment," and "loss of home" read as distant concepts in a history textbook. This film makes them concrete: a child's expression, a family that does not know what they are allowed to take or where they are going.It also reminds us of something unsettling. Belonging is not secure. It can be revoked overnight, by war, by panic, by a racialized gaze. Not because you did anything wrong, but because you looked like a certain kind of person. If this film makes you uneasy, I think that unease is real and worth sitting with. It is telling you that the state has never treated everyone who lives here the same way. Understanding that history is part of living here too.

You Are on Indian Land

SYNOPSIS / 影片简介

1969年的经典短片，记录了发生在安大略省考尔纳瓦加保留地的封锁抗议。影片直接展示了抗议者与警方之间的法律与道德交锋。这是一部关于权利觉醒的早期影像记录，简洁有力地传达了“土地”在身份构建中的核心地位。

CURATOR'S NOTE / 策展笔记

本片的标题本身就是一个强大的政治宣言。在策展过程中，我选择这部影片是希望观众能够思考：当“法律”与“正义”发生冲突时，影像该站在哪一边？对于新移民来说，理解“Indian Land”这一概念是解构加拿大身份政治的第一步。这段占位文字旨在模拟一种学术性的引导，探讨影像作为社会干预工具的可能性。通过将这部早期的黑白纪录片与现代叙事对比，我们可以清晰地看到权力的运作逻辑是如何通过空间隔离实现的。这里的策展分析将帮助观众从情感共鸣转向深度的社会学思考。
